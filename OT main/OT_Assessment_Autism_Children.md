# Occupational Therapy — Comprehensive Assessment Guide for Autistic Children

---

## PHASE 1: INTAKE & REFERRAL INFORMATION

- Who referred the child? (Pediatrician, neurologist, school, self-referral by parent)
- Reason for referral (specific concerns)
- Previous assessments or diagnoses
- Date of referral vs. date of first appointment (delays, urgency)
- Insurance/funding source (impacts session frequency)

---

## PHASE 2: BIRTH & PRENATAL HISTORY

### Prenatal History
- Was the pregnancy planned?
- Any infections during pregnancy (TORCH: Toxoplasmosis, Rubella, CMV, Herpes)
- Medications taken during pregnancy (antidepressants, anticonvulsants, etc.)
- Alcohol, smoking, or drug exposure during pregnancy
- Gestational diabetes or hypertension
- Any fetal distress noted during scans?
- Number of prenatal check-ups
- Any abnormalities on ultrasound?
- Was it a single or multiple pregnancy (twins, triplets)?
- Maternal age at delivery
- Paternal age at conception

### Birth History
- Type of delivery:
  - Normal vaginal delivery (NVD)
  - Caesarean section (elective or emergency) — reason?
  - Assisted delivery (forceps, vacuum/ventouse)
- Gestation at birth (term = 37–42 weeks; preterm < 37 weeks; post-term > 42 weeks)
- Birth weight (low birth weight < 2.5 kg?)
- APGAR score at 1 minute and 5 minutes
- Did the baby cry immediately at birth?
- Was resuscitation needed?
- Any jaundice (neonatal hyperbilirubinemia)?
- NICU admission — duration and reason?
- Any birth injuries (brachial plexus, cephalohematoma)?
- Was meconium present in the amniotic fluid?
- Cord complications (nuchal cord, cord prolapse)?
- Blood group incompatibility (ABO/Rh)?

### Neonatal Period
- Was the baby able to feed (breast/bottle) soon after birth?
- Rooting reflex, sucking reflex present?
- Any seizures in the neonatal period?
- Temperature regulation problems?
- Hypoglycemia in the newborn?

## PHASE 3: DEVELOPMENTAL HISTORY

### Motor Milestones
- Neck holding: expected ~3 months
- Rolling over: ~4–5 months
- Sitting without support: ~6–8 months
- Crawling (did the child crawl, or skip crawling?): ~8–10 months
- Standing with support: ~9–10 months
- Independent standing: ~11–12 months
- Independent walking: ~12–15 months
- Running: ~18 months
- Climbing stairs (both feet one step / alternating): 2–3 years
- Jumping with both feet: ~2 years
- Hopping on one foot: ~4 years

### Fine Motor Milestones
- Palmar grasp: ~3–4 months
- Pincer grasp (thumb + forefinger): ~9–10 months
- Building a tower of 2–3 blocks: ~15–18 months
- Scribbling: ~18 months
- Drawing a circle: ~3 years
- Drawing a cross: ~4 years
- Drawing a triangle: ~5 years
- Holding a crayon/pencil: how (fisted grip, digital pronate, tripod)?
- Able to use scissors (with assistance / independently)?
- Buttoning and unbuttoning clothes
- Tying shoelaces

### Language & Communication Milestones
- Cooing: ~2 months
- Babbling (consonant-vowel sounds): ~6 months
- "Mama/Dada" non-specifically: ~8–9 months
- First meaningful single word: expected by 12 months
- Two-word phrases: ~18–24 months
- Three-word sentences: ~24–30 months
- Regression — did the child lose speech or skills they had previously?
- Is speech used functionally or echolalia (immediate/delayed)?
- Does the child use gestures (pointing, waving)?
- Does the child respond to their name when called?
- Does the child initiate conversation?

### Social Milestones
- Social smile: ~6 weeks
- Stranger anxiety: ~6–8 months
- Joint attention (pointing to share interest with another person): ~9–14 months
- Imitation of actions and sounds
- Parallel play, associative play, cooperative play — which stage?
- Eye contact — spontaneous or absent?
- Does the child show affection to caregivers?
- Separation anxiety — typical or extreme?

### Cognitive Milestones
- Object permanence: ~8–12 months
- Symbolic/pretend play: ~18 months
- Cause-and-effect understanding
- Matching, sorting, categorization
- Understanding of "mine" and "yours" (possessiveness)
- Recognizing own name in writing, letters, numbers

### Self-Care Milestones
- Feeding self with fingers: ~12 months
- Spoon use: ~18 months
- Fork use: ~24 months
- Drinking from open cup: ~12–18 months
- Toilet training — age started, current status (daytime/nighttime)
- Dressing: undressing before dressing; sequence of clothing
- Bathing: tolerance, independent steps
- Toothbrushing: tolerance, technique

## PHASE 4: MEDICAL & HEALTH HISTORY

### Diagnoses
- Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) — when diagnosed, by whom?
- Intellectual Disability (ID) — IQ level if tested
- ADHD — combined, inattentive, or hyperactive-impulsive type
- Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD)
- Epilepsy / seizure disorder (type, frequency, medication)
- Anxiety disorders
- Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD)
- Gastrointestinal issues (constipation, GERD, food allergies)
- Sleep disorders (insomnia, night waking, sleep apnea)
- Genetic conditions (Down Syndrome, Fragile X, Angelman, Rett, Tuberous Sclerosis)
- Hearing impairment — type and degree?
- Visual impairment — refractive error, cortical visual impairment?

### Medications
- Current medications (name, dose, frequency, duration)
- Medications tried and discontinued — reason?
- Any behavioral changes noted with medication?
- Vitamins/supplements (B6, magnesium, omega-3, melatonin)?

### Surgeries & Hospitalizations
- Any surgeries (ear tubes/grommets, tonsillectomy, orthopedic)?
- Duration and reason for hospitalization
- Response to anesthesia

### Immunization History
- Up to date per schedule?
- Any reactions post-vaccination?

### Allergies
- Food allergies (specify — gluten, casein, nuts, eggs)?
- Environmental allergies (dust, pollen)?
- Drug allergies?

## PHASE 5: FAMILY HISTORY

- Any family member diagnosed with ASD, ADHD, or learning disabilities?
- Family history of intellectual disability
- Family history of psychiatric disorders (schizophrenia, bipolar, OCD, depression)
- Family history of speech/language delays
- Family history of genetic disorders
- Family history of epilepsy
- Consanguinity (are parents related by blood)?
- Parental education level and occupation
- Sibling details (ages, any special needs?)
- Who is the primary caregiver?
- Any recent family stressors (divorce, death, relocation, financial stress)?

---

## PHASE 6: SOCIAL & ENVIRONMENTAL HISTORY

- Type of home (house/apartment, urban/rural)
- Number of people living in the home
- Access to outdoor play spaces
- Screen time per day (type: TV, tablet, phone)
- Childcare or school type (mainstream, special needs, home-schooled)
- Support systems available (extended family, community)
- Cultural or religious factors that may affect therapy
- Financial barriers to therapy attendance
- Transportation access

## PHASE 7: BEHAVIORAL OBSERVATIONS (CLINICAL INTERVIEW WITH CAREGIVERS)

### Sensory Profile Questions

**Tactile (Touch)**
- Does the child resist light touch but tolerate deep pressure?
- Does the child avoid certain textures of clothing (tags, seams, specific fabrics)?
- Does the child dislike being touched by others (especially unexpected touch)?
- Does the child seek out tactile stimulation (rubbing surfaces, touching everything)?
- Does the child have difficulty with grooming (haircuts, nail cutting, face washing)?
- Any food texture aversions (refuses soft/lumpy/crunchy/mixed textures)?
- Does the child walk barefoot or refuse to walk barefoot?
- Does the child react strongly to pain differently than expected (over- or under-reactive)?

**Proprioception (Body Position & Pressure)**
- Does the child seek heavy work (pushing, pulling, carrying, crashing into furniture)?
- Does the child hug too tightly?
- Does the child chew on non-food items (pencils, clothing, hands)?
- Does the child have poor awareness of how much force they use (breaks toys, pushes friends too hard)?
- Does the child lean against walls or people frequently?
- Does the child prefer wearing tight clothing or weighted blankets?

**Vestibular (Movement & Balance)**
- Does the child seek excessive movement (spinning, swinging, rocking)?
- Does the child become dizzy easily or not at all with spinning?
- Does the child fear heights or movement (car sickness, fear of swings)?
- Does the child have poor balance (frequent falls)?
- Does the child rock their body when sitting?
- Does the child avoid playground equipment?

**Auditory (Sound)**
- Does the child cover their ears in response to certain sounds?
- Sounds that trigger distress (vacuum, hand dryer, fire alarm, crowd noise)?
- Does the child seem to not hear when called but respond to other sounds (selective hearing)?
- Does the child seek loud sounds or make loud noises?
- Is the child distracted by background noise?

**Visual**
- Does the child look at objects from unusual angles?
- Does the child stare at lights or spinning objects?
- Does the child cover one eye or squint frequently?
- Is the child distracted by visual clutter?
- Does the child line up objects rather than play with them?
- Does the child have difficulty with visual tracking (following a moving object)?

**Olfactory (Smell)**
- Does the child smell food before eating it?
- Does the child smell people, objects, or environments excessively?
- Does the child react strongly to certain smells (perfume, cleaning products)?

**Gustatory (Taste)**
- Does the child have an extremely limited food repertoire?
- Does the child refuse foods based on taste, texture, temperature, or appearance?
- Does the child gag or vomit in response to certain foods?
- Does the child crave very spicy or very bland foods?

**Interoception (Internal Body Signals)**
- Does the child recognize when they are hungry or full?
- Does the child recognize when they need to use the toilet?
- Does the child recognize pain (may seem not to feel pain, or may overreact)?
- Does the child recognize fatigue, fear, or anxiety in their body?

### Behavioral Questions
- Does the child engage in self-stimulatory behaviors (stimming)? Specify (hand-flapping, rocking, spinning, finger-flickering, vocalizations)
- Does the child have rigid routines? What happens when routines are disrupted?
- Does the child have intense, narrow interests?
- Does the child have aggressive behaviors (hitting, biting, kicking)? Triggers?
- Does the child engage in self-injurious behavior (head-banging, biting self)?
- Does the child have difficulty with transitions between activities?
- What is the child's typical response to "no"?
- Does the child demonstrate frustration tolerance?
- Does the child have meltdowns vs. shutdowns — how does each manifest?

### Attention & Executive Function
- How long can the child attend to a preferred activity?
- How long for a non-preferred activity?
- Does the child shift attention easily between tasks?
- Can the child follow multi-step instructions?
- Does the child plan and sequence tasks (getting dressed, packing bag)?
- Does the child initiate tasks independently or need prompting?
- Does the child have difficulty stopping an activity to move on?

## PHASE 8: STANDARDIZED ASSESSMENTS USED IN OT FOR AUTISM

### Sensory Assessments
1. **Sensory Profile 2** (Winnie Dunn) — caregiver questionnaire, ages 0–14+ years; categorizes sensory processing patterns
2. **Sensory Processing Measure (SPM / SPM-P)** — home and school forms
3. **Short Sensory Profile (SSP)** — abbreviated screener
4. **DeGangi-Berk Test of Sensory Integration** — ages 3–5 years
5. **Test of Sensory Integration (TSI)**

### Motor Assessments
6. **Bruininks-Oseretsky Test of Motor Proficiency, 2nd Ed. (BOT-2)** — ages 4–21; fine and gross motor skills
7. **Movement Assessment Battery for Children, 2nd Ed. (MABC-2)** — ages 3–16; motor impairment
8. **Peabody Developmental Motor Scales, 2nd Ed. (PDMS-2)** — ages 0–5; gross and fine motor
9. **Developmental Test of Visual-Motor Integration (VMI / Beery-VMI)** — copying geometric forms
10. **Test of Visual Perceptual Skills, 4th Ed. (TVPS-4)** — non-motor visual perception

### Adaptive Behavior & Function
11. **Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales, 3rd Ed. (Vineland-3)** — communication, daily living, socialization, motor
12. **Pediatric Evaluation of Disability Inventory (PEDI / PEDI-CAT)** — functional skills and caregiver assistance
13. **WeeFIM (Functional Independence Measure for Children)** — self-care, mobility, cognition
14. **Canadian Occupational Performance Measure (COPM)** — client-centered goal setting with caregiver

### Play Assessments
15. **Test of Playfulness (ToP)** — observational measure of playfulness
16. **Revised Knox Preschool Play Scale (RKPPS)** — space, material, imitation, participation
17. **Transdisciplinary Play-Based Assessment (TPBA)** — multidisciplinary, observational

### Handwriting Assessments
18. **Evaluation Tool of Children's Handwriting (ETCH)** — manuscript and cursive
19. **Print Tool (Handwriting Without Tears)** — legibility, pencil grip, letter formation
20. **Minnesota Handwriting Assessment (MHA)**

### Visual Motor / Perception
21. **Beery VMI** — visual-motor integration
22. **Motor-Free Visual Perception Test (MVPT-4)** — visual perception without motor involvement
23. **Developmental Test of Visual Perception (DTVP-3)**

### Autism-Specific Tools (used in collaborative assessment)
24. **Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, 2nd Ed. (ADOS-2)** — gold standard observational tool (used by psychologists, some OTs trained)
25. **Childhood Autism Rating Scale (CARS-2)** — severity rating
26. **Social Responsiveness Scale, 2nd Ed. (SRS-2)** — social functioning caregiver report

### Cognitive / Developmental Screening (collaborative)
27. **Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development, 4th Ed. (Bayley-4)** — ages 0–42 months
28. **Wechsler Preschool and Primary Scale of Intelligence (WPPSI-IV)** — ages 2.5–7
29. **Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC-V)** — ages 6–16

## PHASE 9: CLINICAL OBSERVATION PROCEDURES (IN-SESSION)

### Postural Control & Core Stability
- Can the child sit upright without back support?
- Does the child W-sit, slouch, or prop?
- Does the child fatigue quickly when sitting at a desk?
- Can the child maintain midline orientation?
- Assess trunk rotation and righting reactions

### Bilateral Coordination
- Can the child use both hands together (cutting with scissors, opening jars)?
- Does the child have an established hand dominance?
- Does the child cross the midline comfortably?

### Muscle Tone
- Observe for hypotonia (low tone — floppy, poor endurance) or hypertonia (high tone — stiffness, limited range)
- Assess shoulder stability, grip strength
- Observe gait pattern (toe-walking, wide base)

### Reflex Integration
- Check for persistence of primitive reflexes that should have integrated:
  - Moro reflex (startle) — should integrate by 4–6 months
  - Asymmetric Tonic Neck Reflex (ATNR) — by 6 months
  - Symmetrical Tonic Neck Reflex (STNR) — by 9–11 months
  - Palmar grasp reflex — by 5–6 months
  - Plantar grasp reflex — by 9–12 months
  - Tonic Labyrinthine Reflex (TLR) — by 6 months

### Pencil Grasp & Handwriting
- Type of grip: fisted, digital pronate, quadrupod, tripod (static vs. dynamic)
- Pencil pressure (too heavy / too light)
- Paper stability (does the child use the helper hand?)
- Letter formation — starting points, directionality
- Spacing between words and letters
- Letter size consistency
- Line adherence

### Visual Motor Integration (Copying Tasks)
- Copy simple shapes (circle, cross, triangle, square, diamond — developmental sequence)
- Copy designs from Beery VMI test

### Scissor Skills
- Holding posture (thumb up?)
- Opening and closing action
- Ability to cut on a line (straight, curved, geometric shapes)

### Fine Motor Dexterity Tasks
- Pegboard tasks (speed and accuracy)
- Block stacking
- String beading
- Manipulation of fasteners (buttons, zippers, snaps)
- Coin dropping into a slot (in-hand manipulation)

### Sensory Observation During Session
- Observe reaction to the OT room environment (lights, sounds, smells)
- Note self-regulatory behaviors during assessment
- Observe emotional regulation when frustrated or transitioning
- Note sensory-seeking or sensory-avoiding behaviors during free play

## PHASE 10: SCHOOL & CLASSROOM FUNCTIONAL ASSESSMENT

- Can the child sit in a group at circle time?
- Can the child transition between activities with the class?
- Can the child manage their belongings (school bag, lunch box, water bottle)?
- Can the child perform classroom fine motor tasks (coloring, cutting, pasting)?
- Can the child follow classroom routines?
- How does the child behave in the cafeteria (noise, food)?
- What accommodations are currently in place?
- Does the child have an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or 504 Plan?

---

## PHASE 11: ACTIVITIES OF DAILY LIVING (ADL) ASSESSMENT

### Feeding
- Food repertoire: how many foods accepted? (less than 20 = food selectivity concern)
- Textures accepted (puree, soft, crunchy, mixed)
- Temperature preferences
- Utensil use (spoon, fork, chopsticks?)
- Is mealtime a positive or stressful experience?
- Does the child eat with the family?
- Duration of mealtimes

### Dressing
- Can the child identify front and back of clothing?
- Can the child pull on/off t-shirts, pants, socks, shoes?
- Can the child manage fasteners (velcro, buttons, zippers, snaps, laces)?
- Sequence of dressing — does the child know the order?
- Does the child resist certain clothing items? Why?

### Bathing & Hygiene
- Tolerates bathing? Specific aversions (water on face, shampoo, soap)?
- Can the child wash their own body with prompting?
- Toothbrushing tolerance (texture of bristle, flavor of toothpaste, duration)
- Hair brushing/washing — reaction?
- Nail cutting — reaction?
- Face washing — reaction?

### Toileting
- Currently toilet trained (day/night)?
- Can the child recognize urge to void/defecate?
- Can the child manage clothing for toileting independently?
- Any history of constipation or bowel issues?
- Does the child wipe adequately?
- Does the child flush without distress?
- Does the child use public toilets? Any barriers (noise of flush, smell, crowds)?

### Sleep
- Sleep schedule (bedtime, wake time)
- Sleep onset — time taken to fall asleep
- Night wakings — frequency and duration
- Does the child co-sleep?
- Sleep hygiene routine (bath, book, dark room, white noise)?
- Use of melatonin or other sleep aids?
- Impact of poor sleep on daytime functioning

## PHASE 12: GOAL SETTING

### Canadian Occupational Performance Measure (COPM) — Questions Asked
- "What are the daily activities your child has difficulty with?"
- "Which of these problems are most important to address first?"
- "How well does your child perform this activity on a scale of 1–10?"
- "How satisfied are you with their current performance on a scale of 1–10?"

### Goal Areas Typically Targeted in OT for Autism
- Sensory regulation strategies
- Fine motor skill development
- Self-care independence (feeding, dressing, hygiene)
- Handwriting / pre-writing skills
- Visual motor integration
- Primitive reflex integration
- Emotional regulation
- Transition management
- Play skills
- Social participation
- School readiness / classroom function
- Executive function support

## PHASE 13: INTERVENTION APPROACHES USED IN OT FOR AUTISM

- **Ayres Sensory Integration (ASI)** — sensory-rich, child-led, in a gym with suspended equipment; targets sensory modulation and motor planning
- **Sensory Diet** — individualized schedule of sensory activities throughout the day
- **DIR/Floortime** — relationship-based, child-led approach following the child's developmental level
- **Cognitive Orientation to daily Occupational Performance (CO-OP)** — problem-solving and metacognition for older children
- **PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System)** — used collaboratively with SLT for functional communication
- **Visual Supports** — visual schedules, first-then boards, task strips, timers
- **Graduated Exposure / Desensitization** — for sensory aversions (feeding, grooming)
- **Handwriting Without Tears (HWT)** — multisensory handwriting program
- **Sequential Oral Sensory (SOS) Approach to Feeding** — systematic feeding therapy
- **Social Stories** — to prepare for transitions and social situations
- **Primitive Reflex Integration Exercises** — Masgutova Neurosensorimotor Reflex Integration (MNRI)
- **Weighted Vests / Compression Clothing** — proprioceptive input (used with caution and evidence)
- **Environmental Modifications** — seating, lighting, noise, workspace organization

## PHASE 14: PARENT/CAREGIVER EDUCATION & HOME PROGRAM

- Explanation of sensory profile results
- How to implement a sensory diet at home
- Home program exercises (printed or video-based)
- Strategies for meltdown prevention and co-regulation
- Tips for ADL management (feeding, dressing, hygiene)
- How to use visual supports at home
- Guidance for school communication and IEP input
- Caregiver self-care — parent stress and burnout screening

---

## PHASE 15: REPORTING & MULTIDISCIPLINARY COLLABORATION

### OT Report Includes:
- Background information
- Assessment tools used and rationale
- Results and clinical observations
- Interpretation (areas of strength and difficulty)
- Functional impact on daily life
- Goals (SMART format)
- Recommendations for therapy, school, and home
- Frequency of therapy recommended

### Collaboration With:
- Speech-Language Therapist (SLT) — communication, feeding, oral motor
- Physiotherapist (PT) — gross motor, gait, tone
- Psychologist — diagnosis, behavior, cognitive assessment
- Developmental Pediatrician — medical management, medication
- Special Educator — academic and curriculum support
- ABA Therapist — behavior intervention planning
- Nutritionist/Dietitian — food selectivity, nutrition
- Social Worker — family support, access to services

---

## PHASE 16: REVIEW & REASSESSMENT

- Frequency of formal re-assessment: typically every 6–12 months
- Re-administer standardized tools to measure progress
- Review and update goals with caregivers
- Transition planning (preschool → primary school → secondary)
- Discharge planning criteria
